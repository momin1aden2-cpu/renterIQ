Our Story

We built this because
we've been there.

A bond dispute. No evidence. A lesson that every Australian renter deserves better tools than a paper form on a Saturday morning.

I found the apartment on a Thursday. By Saturday I had signed the lease. Two-bedroom in Melbourne's inner north — nothing fancy, but it felt like home. I did the entry inspection with the property manager, ticked the boxes on the paper form they handed me, handed it back, and thought nothing more of it.

I lived there for fourteen months. I was a good tenant. Paid rent on time. Reported a leaking tap within a week of it starting. Swept the floors before I left. I thought I'd done everything right.

Then the bond claim arrived.

$1,840 for carpet replacement in the main bedroom. $320 for repainting a wall in the hallway. They said it was damage I'd caused. I knew it wasn't — the carpet had been worn when I moved in, and that mark on the wall was there on day one. I remembered it clearly.

But remembering something and proving something are completely different things.

I had no photos from move-in. The entry report I'd signed was the agency's version, checked off in a rush on a Saturday morning. I had nothing timestamped, nothing room-by-room, nothing that could stand up against the agency's claim.

I lost $2,160 of my bond. Not because I'd caused any damage. But because I didn't have the tools to prove I hadn't.

That experience didn't just cost money. It changed how I thought about renting.

Every Australian renter has the right to get their bond back if they've looked after a property. But the system is built around whoever has the better paperwork. Agencies have professional condition reports. Renters have whatever they had time to write down on a Saturday morning. That imbalance is what RenterIQ is built to fix.

I spent the months after that dispute learning everything I could about how bond claims work, what evidence tribunals accept, and what renters are actually entitled to under Australian tenancy law. What I found was consistent across every state: the outcome almost always follows the evidence trail.

So I started building the tool I wished I'd had. Something that would walk you through a property room by room, photograph what matters, timestamp it all, and generate a professional report — before you ever hand the entry form back. Something that would read your lease in plain English before you signed it. Something that would store everything in one place, so fourteen months later you'd have everything you needed.

That tool is RenterIQ. Built for the renter who works hard, pays their rent, and deserves to get their full bond back.
